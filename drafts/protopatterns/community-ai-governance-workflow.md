# Community Governance Workflow for AI Model Release

**ID:** P-002
**Type:** Process
**Description:** A structured community sign-off and governance workflow for AI model selection, review, and release in Indigenous and community-sensitive HASS contexts, grounded in OCAP and CARE principles, with defined decision-making responsibilities, documentation standards, review stages, and community feedback loops.
**Created:** 2026-03-16
**Last updated:** 2026-03-16

## Notes
### From: Interview: RSE/AI-ML Engineer, Centre for Digital Humanities and Social Analysis, 2026-03-16

**What the framework involves:**
> "I initiated the development of a review stage where both researchers and community representatives would collaboratively evaluate model proposals. This included a checklist focused on key questions: Is the model's training data transparent? How does it align with the community's understanding of their history? Is there a clear mechanism for feedback and accountability?"

> "We needed a structured approach to assess not just the technical aspects of the model but also how it aligned with community values and expectations... incorporating OCAP principles and the CARE Principles for Indigenous data governance."

**Concrete steps described:**
1. Initial consultations with community representatives — establish mutual understanding of goals and expectations
2. Collaborative evaluation of model proposals (researchers + community representatives)
3. Co-development of model cards and other documentation artefacts
4. Regular review stages where community feedback is actively sought and integrated
5. Structured feedback mechanisms that communicate decisions back to community and create avenues for ongoing dialogue

**What's currently missing (the governance gap):**
> "There's ambiguity about who holds responsibility for decisions regarding model release — whether it's the researchers, the institution, or the community... Documentation standards are also sorely lacking. There's no consensus on what specific information needs to be recorded for model cards or community sign-off records... The review and approval workflow is another area that needs development. Currently, we often revert to informal processes that can be time-consuming and may not adequately capture community input."

**Readiness conditions — when this framework fits:**
> "Existing Relationships: Teams that have already established trust with the communities they're working with are more likely to embrace the framework."
> "Institutional Support: Leadership that prioritizes ethical research practices and fosters a culture of collaboration."
> "Alignment with Research Goals: Projects that inherently aim for social impact, community benefit, or participatory methodologies."

**Contexts where it faces resistance:**
> "High-Pressure Environments: Projects driven solely by funding timelines or deliverables that emphasize speed over relationship-building."
> "Technical Focus: Teams that prioritize technical outputs without considering the social implications."
> "Lack of Awareness or Training: Disciplines where ethical engagement is not part of the standard discourse."

**Institutional support — what it looks like in practice:**
- Financial: budget for community consultation travel, stipends for community members, hiring a community liaison
- Timeline: explicit permission from leadership to extend timelines for community engagement
- Implicit support ("permission to engage thoughtfully without the practical support") is less effective but still helps

**Advocating to stakeholders — framing that works:**
> "I framed my argument by emphasizing the ethical necessity of community engagement, but I also highlighted the risks associated with proceeding without thorough consultation. I pointed out that ignoring community concerns could lead to reputational damage for the university and the research team."
- Reputational risk framing more effective than ethical obligation framing
- "Long-term investment in the sustainability of the project and its outcomes"

**Hard limit — the funding withdrawal:**
In the climate policy project, a funder insisted on using unconsented scraped data. The team withdrew from the funding rather than compromise. Short-term: uncertainty, questions from other funders. Long-term: stronger reputation with mission-aligned funders, clearer team identity.

**Key insight on embedding vs. retrofitting:**
> "These approaches work best when they're embedded into the team's DNA early — not bolted on later. Whether it's OCAP/CARE principles, model cards, community co-researchers, or reflexivity practices, they're most effective when they shape how the project is conceived from the start, not when they're retrofitted."

**Relationship to other patterns:**
- P-001 (Training Data Provenance Audit) — a specific component of this workflow
- D-001 (Community-Co-Developed Model Cards) — the documentation artefact produced by this workflow
- The broader framework is explicitly grounded in OCAP (Ownership, Control, Access, Possession) and CARE (Collective Benefit, Authority to Control, Responsibility, Ethical Relationships) principles

**HASS specificity:**
Strongest evidence in Indigenous/community-governed data contexts. Broader HASS adoption (e.g. social media research, humanities) is possible but adoption is variable — many RSEs don't see OCAP/CARE as relevant to non-Indigenous work. This is identified as a gap.
